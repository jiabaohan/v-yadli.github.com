Programming languages

Yeah I worry about this.

More than 1 year ago I migrated from the wild to .Net, the managed safehouse. I got very comfortable with C#, created a lot of stuff that I could never do before.

Talking about expressive power, I personally think C# is good.
Talking about performance, it is also on par, or even significantly outperforms with the major platforms. Except some of the native languages. Especially after my effort to dismiss the boundary jumping penalty between two worlds.

But now, the question is, I'm lying safely in my comfort zone(and I'm not going to get harmed if I don't stand up and do something),then why bother jumping out? Isn't it good to enjoy what you have, and you are good at, and focus on the things you can create? What is the ultimate goal to acquire skills for new programming languages?

Or sometimes it's not even new. I may want to take one step back and look at the tools I once used, but now staying somewhere on my hard drive, in my head, in a peaceful and sealed corner, rusted, and covered with dust. Especially, for today(2013-04-17) I saw someone building a quite decent indy game with QBasic. God. My mother tongue.

http://www.pcworld.com/article/2033318/black-annex-is-the-best-qbasic-game-youve-ever-seen.html

I'll never write such a game with C#. Even with the most powerful IDE, the most wonderful editor, the most friendly graphics/gaming engines right at my dispose, I simply won't. Perhaps because I don't want, or perhaps I simply can't. It's gone retro-style, and I could never hold that mindset that I used 10+ years ago while casting spells from a dialectal language in the C family. I still clearly remember. Back then it was all that simple. Think, write, execute. With my current toolchain, everything is tinted with the dark brown color of industry: yeah you've got to use an IDE, otherwise you cannot get a clue about the programming interface and their behaviour. You've got to leave your browser locked down in your main memory, because you will always encounter a weird piece of hardware that is hard to talk with. Dependencies. To understand X, you must first understand Y. Then Z. Then a chain. Then a tree. Then a graph, where you get lost, exhausted well before you even start to draw a circle on your screen.

Think, write, execute. A simple block of code that I use to display my character. Then I need some variables for him. Health points. Inventory. All I can think of, I write it down, somewhere in a very large BASIC file. And at last I recall that there's another code block that put the drawing logic of the current character into a buffer, then blitting them onscreen. Everything laid down linearly in a comprehensive way just like the bare bone memory mapping scheme of any computer.

I'm not saying that I don't like the current state of my mind. Rather it's just an example. It's not that I become slow on C#. It's for game making. I simply cannot implement (and as a consequence, think, due to limited human memory) as fast as writing with BASIC like a sketchpad. Of course, I would never write any distributed key value stores with BASIC, for different but yet sensibly similar reasons.

I wasn't not born for C#. Rather, for something I want to build. So the problem hiding behind the question is now revealed. I'm stuck with .Net's capabilities. Then I'll write a game like writing a web server. Too bad. The reason is showing. I must jump out. Open my old toolbox. Touch and feel my old companions. Seek for new borns, or the polished, and extract the traits, the ideas, the stories behind.
